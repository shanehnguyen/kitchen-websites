## THE DOCTRINE (what 3 research passes proved)

1. **Ugly/native wins.** Raw, founder-shot, lo-fi creative outperforms polish on Meta — native content reduces ad-filter resistance; the interrupt must be unexpected, not pretty. (Multiple 2025–26 sources.)
2. **Statics win direct response; video wins awareness/retargeting.** Image ads produce higher CTRs and lower CPAs for direct response — supports the static-heavy launch.
3. **The 5 hooks that dominate $1M+/month accounts** (Motion 2026 Benchmarks; 550k ads, ~$1.3B spend): **Confession · Bold Claim · Relatability · Contrast · Curiosity.** Every launch ad below maps to at least one.
4. **The 5-part contractor ad structure** (validated across home-services accounts): Hook → Agitate → Offer → Proof → CTA. Proof = a number or a name, every time.
5. **Specificity beats generality** ("$7.2M" beats "millions"; "72 hours" beats "fast").
6. **Before/after is the trade's native visual language.** The buyer thinks in before/afters — use his grammar.
7. **RISKY ≠ FAKE.** Offense rides on documents, enemies, and tough love — never costumes. The avatar's scam filter is his dominant screen; fabrication is the one unforgivable aesthetic.

## THE CTA ARCHITECTURE (locked)

- **All cold ads → the Score** ("See how you stack up — takes 3 minutes"). Never "book a call" cold; never the Rescue offer cold (Godfather Offers are for the 3% — the Score catches the 37%).
- One destination = clean data at $50/day.
- **Congruence rule:** rivalry-angle ads match the Score's comparison frame natively. Burn-angle ads (FTC/Houzz/Angi) need the bridge line: *"See where you actually stand — without the platforms. 3 minutes."*
- Retargeting only (wk 3+): Grader head-to-head CTA, book-a-call CTA.
- **Tested variable, not a launch change:** Meta lead forms convert 25–35% higher than landing pages for contractors — but the Score page IS the strategy (diagnosis + data capture). Honor the page; test an instant-form variant in wave 2 only if Score-page CPL disappoints.
- **Comment duty:** polarizing ads generate arguments; the comments are distribution ONLY if Jack shows up and wins them same-day.

# THE RANKED BOARD

## S-TIER — THE LAUNCH 7 (5 statics + 2 videos)

**1. The Contrast Static — "Your kitchens are better. His Google is better."**
*NEW this pass. Hook type: Contrast + Bold Claim (Motion #4/#2).*
Visual: split image — left, a gorgeous kitchen (his craft); right, a Google review panel showing "4 reviews" vs "87 reviews." Headline: *"Your kitchens are better. His Google is better. Guess who got the call?"*
Why #1-caliber: it compresses the entire dossier into nine words — craft pride, injustice, the rivalry wound, the verification mechanism. Perfect Score congruence.
Stress notes: use a real (own-portfolio or licensed) kitchen photo, never a competitor's work; review-panel graphic must be a generic mockup, not a real business's screenshot.

**2. The Map-Pack Screenshot — "Are you in it?"**
*Hook: Curiosity + Contrast.*
Real local 3-pack, competitors blurred: *"This is what a homeowner sees when they search 'kitchen remodeler near me' in your town. Are you in it?"*
Why: instantly understood, personally threatening, native screenshot aesthetic, costs nothing, perfectly congruent.
Stress notes: blur all business identities; rotate city names in copy for freshness.

**3. The Real-News Clipping — the FTC story.**
*Jack's "ex-Google" instinct, rehabilitated. Hook: Curiosity + receipts.*
Static styled as a news clipping — headline, dateline: *"FTC Orders HomeAdvisor to Pay $7.2 Million Over Lead-Selling Practices."* Primary text: "You probably paid for some of those leads. Here's how to never need them."
Why: full clickbait-news scroll-stop with zero fabrication — the news is real and federal.
Stress notes: bridge line mandatory (burn-angle → comparison page); style it as A clipping, don't impersonate a specific outlet's trademark dress.

**4. The Plain-Text Callout.**
*Hook: Relatability + Bold Claim. The banker.*
White background, black text: *"I do websites, Google, and SEO for kitchen & bath businesses. That's it. That's the ad."*
Why: maximum anti-design pattern interrupt; copy does the targeting; reads human.
Stress notes: low emotional voltage — works by relevance; never run it alone, pair with hotter ads.

**5. The Nephew.**
*Hook: Relatability + Confession-adjacent humor.*
Static, plain text or simple photo of a sad website on a laptop: *"Your nephew's website is costing you $24,000 a job. He did his best. Fire him anyway."*
Why: names a real character in the buyer's life; the humor is his humor.
Stress notes: tone stays affectionate ("he did his best") — sneering reads punching-down; $24k figure sourced in primary text (median kitchen job).

## KILLED (and why — so the corpses stay buried)

- **Fake ex-Google employee / fabricated insider** — scam costume + Meta policy bait. (The FORMAT survives as #3, the real-news clipping.)
- **Fabricated client emails / testimonials / text threads** — fake-proof aesthetic; the avatar's filter doesn't parse "dramatization."
- **Founder-likeness imagery (Google founders etc.)** — implied endorsement, near-certain rejection.
- **The Obituary static** — marketer-clever, not owner-resonant; fails the corn test. Mechanism survives in V3.
- **The King Kong doodle** — works on marketing-literate audiences; reads unserious to a showroom owner.
- **"Agencies HATE this trick" grammar** — the avatar's scam filter exists specifically for this sentence.

# VIDEO DESCRIPTIONS (production-level)

**V1 — THE TEARDOWN (Launch)**
*Format: screen-recording + voiceover. 60–90 sec. Hook: Curiosity + Contrast.*
Opens cold on the most damning finding — no intro, no logo: screen shows an anonymized showroom's Google profile, cursor circling "3 reviews" while VO says: "This kitchen showroom does beautiful work. Google has no idea." Next 50 seconds: rapid-fire walk through the profile — missing photos, wrong categories, unanswered review, no posts — each flaw circled on screen, VO naming what it costs in plain trade language ("a homeowner comparing you to the guy with 87 reviews isn't comparing kitchens — she's comparing screens"). Closes on the competitor's stacked profile beside it. CTA card: "Find out how YOURS stacks up — 3 minutes." Production: screen-record + phone-mic VO; polish would hurt it. RULE: anonymized/composite or volunteer profile only — never a named real business without permission.

**V2 — THE DIRECT CALLOUT (Launch)**
*Format: phone-shot talking head, handheld, one take. 30–45 sec. Hook: Relatability.*
You, somewhere real (office, truck, warehouse — not a set): "If you own a kitchen & bath business and your phone's been quieter this year — you're not imagining it. Leads went soft across the industry. But some showrooms are still booked out. The difference isn't their work. It's what homeowners find when they check them out." Beat. "I built a 3-minute check that shows you exactly where you stand against the businesses winning your market. Link's below." Captions burned in (most watch muted). No music, no cuts if avoidable. The rawness IS the trust signal.

**V3 — HOW A REFERRAL DIES (First rotation)**
*Format: POV screen-story. 40 sec. Hook: Contrast + dramatized mechanism.*
POV: phone screen. A text arrives: "You should call [blurred] — they did our kitchen, amazing work." Finger opens Google, types the name. Profile loads: 4 reviews, two photos from 2019, no website link. Thumb hesitates... scrolls down... taps the competitor underneath: 87 reviews, gleaming portfolio. Calls THEM. VO (flat, factual): "This happened to one of your referrals this month. 91% of homeowners check reviews. They decide within 72 hours. Your word of mouth isn't disappearing — it's being intercepted." CTA: the Score. Editing note: the hesitating thumb is the whole ad — make the viewer feel the referral slipping. Corn risk: any explainer-video music or chirpy VO kills it; silence + thumb taps.

**V4 — READING HOUZZ REVIEWS ALOUD (First rotation / campaign two)**
*Format: talking head + on-screen review screenshots. 45–60 sec. Hook: Confession-by-proxy + receipts.*
You, deadpan, reading real public one-star Houzz Pro reviews from kitchen & bath owners — verbatim, flat, no gloating, no commentary between reviews. Three reviews max. Long pause. "I don't sell leads. I make sure the customers you've already earned can actually find you." CTA. RULES: public reviews, shown on screen as receipts, reviewer identities blurred; deadpan is lethal, gloating is corny.

**V5 — PODCAST-CLIP Q&A (Bench / retargeting)**
*Format: mic + captions, podcast-clip aesthetic (honestly framed as founder Q&A — never pretending to be a real show). 30–45 sec per clip; shoot 3 in one session.*
Clip A: "Why do you only work with kitchen & bath businesses?" Clip B: "What actually happened between the FTC and HomeAdvisor?" Clip C: "Why don't you do contracts?" Each answer is an angle compressed to 30 seconds, ending mid-energy (clips that feel excerpted hold attention). Retargeting-first: warm viewers binge these.

**V7 — OWNER-MATH WHITEBOARD (Bench / nurture)**
*Format: phone-shot whiteboard, marker squeaking, one take. 45 sec. Hook: Bold Claim via arithmetic.*
Write "$24,000" big. "Median kitchen job. One." Write the year of FlipFix costs under it. Circle the difference. "You don't need marketing to work miracles. You need it to not lose ONE job a quarter that was already trying to find you." The squeak and the bad handwriting are features — it's how he prices jobs for his own customers.

**V8 — THE MYSTERY SHOPPER (Wave two — the account-carrier if the field results land)**
*Format: documentary-style talking head + call-log graphics. 60–90 sec. Hook: Confession + Bold Claim + original data.*
The setup on camera: "I called 10 kitchen & bath showrooms this week as a homeowner with a $40,000 kitchen budget. Real businesses. Real budget energy. Here's what happened." Then the carnage as on-screen tally: how many answered, how many called back same day, how many EVER called back — against the verified stat that 60% of homeowners decide within 72 hours. Close: "Your marketing isn't your biggest leak. Your phone might be. Find out where you stand." PRODUCTION GATES: comply with state recording law (record only your side or get consent), never name or identify businesses, the findings must be real and reported honestly even if less dramatic than hoped. If the field result is 6-of-10 never called back, this ad runs for a quarter.

**V-NEW (8a) — THE CONFESSION (First rotation, video variant of #8)**
*Format: talking head, slower and quieter than V2. 45 sec. Hook: Confession (Motion's #1).*
"I spent years building websites for kitchen and bath companies. Good websites. And I kept watching the same thing happen: beautiful site, dead phone. It took me embarrassingly long to figure out why — because the answer wasn't the website. It's what a homeowner sees in the ten seconds BEFORE she ever clicks one." → the verification-layer reveal → Score CTA. The admission of having been wrong is the trust mechanism; deliver it like you mean it, because you did live it.

## STANDING RULES (carried across every ad)
1. Every claim documentable — FTC truth-in-advertising applies to FlipFix's own ads; one inflated claim in a referral-dense niche is fatal.
2. No real businesses named or identifiable without written permission (teardowns, before/afters, mystery shopper).
3. Bridge lines on all burn-angle ads (congruence with the comparison-framed Score).
4. Comment sections manned same-day on polarizing ads — the argument is the distribution.
5. Avatar language only: "kitchen & bath businesses / showrooms / remodelers" — never "cabinet dealers" (the filtered-out buyer).
6. Wave cadence: replace fatigued creative every 2–3 weeks from this board, top-ranked first.
